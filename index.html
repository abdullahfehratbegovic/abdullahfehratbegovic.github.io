Automatic Proof Checkers: Can a Computer Verify Mathematical Truth?

Mathematical proofs are often seen as the purest form of human reasoning: precise, logical, and built step by step from fundamental principles. For a long time, it seemed natural to assume that such reasoning could not be fully delegated to machines.

However, the development of automatic proof checkers challenges this intuition in a subtle and fascinating way.


An automatic proof checker is not a system that â€œdiscoversâ€ new mathematics on its own, but one that verifies whether a given proof is logically correct. The key idea is to formalize mathematics to such a degree that every inference step can be checked mechanically.

This requires expressing definitions, axioms, and logical rules in a language precise enough for a computer to understand.

What makes proof checking feasible is the fact that, while finding a proof may require creativity, verifying a proof is often much more systematic. Once a proof is written in a formal system, a computer can check whether each step follows from previous ones according to the rules of logic.

In this sense, proof checkers resemble extremely strict readers that never overlook a gap in reasoning.


Modern proof assistants and checkers, such as those based on type theory or first-order logic, have already been used to verify highly non-trivial results. Their value lies not in replacing mathematicians, but in increasing reliability.

Long and complex proofs can contain subtle errors that are difficult for humans to detect, while machines excel at consistency and precision.

From a broader perspective, automatic proof checking sits at the intersection of mathematics, computer science, and logic. It raises deep questions about the nature of proof, the limits of formalization, and the role of computers in mathematical discovery.

Rather than reducing mathematics to computation, these systems highlight just how rich and structured mathematical reasoning truly is.

Feed-Forward Neural Networks in a Nutshell

A feed-forward neural network is a basic type of artificial neural network where information flows in one direction only â€” from input to output, without loops or feedback.

The network is organized into layers.
The input layer receives raw data (for example, pixel values of an image).
One or more hidden layers process this data by applying weights and biases, followed by an activation function that introduces nonlinearity.
Finally, the output layer produces the networks prediction or classification.

Each neuron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias, and then applies an activation function such as ReLU or sigmoid.
This allows the network to model complex patterns rather than just linear relationships.

Learning happens through training.
The network compares its output to the correct answer using a loss function, which measures error.
This error is then propagated backward through the network using backpropagation, adjusting the weights to reduce future errors.
Despite the name, this backward step is only used during training; during actual use, data still flows forward.

In essence, a feed-forward neural network learns by repeatedly adjusting its internal parameters so that a simple forward computation produces increasingly accurate results.

Space-Filling Curves: When a Line Fills a Square

It might seem impossible for a one-dimensional curve to completely fill a two-dimensional square. A line has length but no area, so intuition suggests gaps must remain. Yet mathematics proves otherwise.

A space-filling curve is a continuous curve whose image passes through every point of a square. The first example was constructed in 1890 by Giuseppe Peano, and later David Hilbert introduced a widely used variation known as the Hilbert curve. These discoveries challenged traditional ideas about dimension and continuity.


How Do They Work?

Space-filling curves rely on recursive construction. Each step divides the square into smaller regions and folds the curve to pass through each one. While finite steps leave gaps, the infinite limit covers the entire square. Formally, these curves are continuous functions from [0,1] into [0,1]^2, but they often lack well-defined tangents almost everywhere.


Dimension and Intuition

These curves show that a one-dimensional domain can behave like a two-dimensional object. They inspired concepts such as topological dimension and Hausdorff dimension, helping mathematicians formalize ideas that intuition alone cannot capture.


Practical Uses

Although initially a theoretical curiosity, space-filling curves have practical applications:
- Computer graphics and image processing to preserve locality,
- Data storage and compression, particularly in multidimensional indexing,
- Scientific computing, including mesh generation and optimization problems.


Lessons from Space-Filling Curves

Space-filling curves demonstrate the power of mathematical rigor. They challenge intuition, reveal hidden possibilities, and remind us that careful definitions and limits can lead to surprising, beautiful results.
